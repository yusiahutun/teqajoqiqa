Outrage as giant balloon hand with middle finger stuck up 'takes modern art too far'

March 29, 2024
blog

With their pickled sharks, unmade beds and paintings made with elephant dung, modern artists have regularly been accused of sticking two fingers up to the traditional art establishment.

But now critics say an exhibition featuring a giant inflatable hand making a similar obscene gesture has gone too far.

The so-called art work, due to go on display at Manchester's award winning Lowry art centre, features a huge balloon in the shape of a hand with it's middle finger raised.

The exhibition, due to be installed in May, will feature a giant middle finger. It has been slammed as a 'diabolical waste of money'

It is part of an £20,000 exhibition designed to capture the "soul of Salford" by British artist Simon Grennan and American Chris Sperandio.

But last night locals said it was "codswallop" and a "diabolical waste of money."

Councillor Bernard Pennington, former mayor of Salford city council, said: "I do not subscribe to this so-called art, which costs a fortune.

"You will get pretentious people who will try to justify it, but this work does not mean anything to anybody. To me it is a load of rubbish and codswallop."

Julie Bates, who has lived in the city for 30 years, added: "It is a diabolical waste of money."

Scroll down for more...

Three giant balloon features, including a head made of bricks, measure 16ft high and 12 ft wide

Measuring some 16ft high and 12ft wide, the hand is one of three balloons designed to float freely inside the Lowry's high-ceilinged promenade gallery, due to be installed in May.

Other balloons in the exhibition, which is being financed by a £20,000 grant from the Esmee Fairbairn Foundation - a private charitable trust - include a head made of bricks and a twisted "Burberry" style MFI sofa.

In an attempt to explain the meaning behind the balloons, Mr Grennan, who spent four days walking around Salford to get inspiration for them, said: "The big hand is the works boss, also the Lottery hand, the hand of God pointing his finger at Salford, or someone giving the finger - it is all in the mix.

"The sofa is about the jaunty nature of life in Salford - there is a swagger among Salford people. The brick head is about the bigness of the place, it is huge. Also Salford has tender men and rock hard women.

"This commission allows us to test our experience of the unique character of Salford and employ our skill and experience to make three major visualisations of the soul of a great international city."

Mark Doyle, curator of special exhibitions at The Lowry, admitted the exhibition would cause "debate" but claimed it was worthwhile.

"The inflatables are emblems of the artists' research into what they felt represented the city," he said. "It is their personal response to the city and we imagine it will cause a lot of debate."
