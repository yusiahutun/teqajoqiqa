Inside The Atelier: Johnstons of Elgin's cashmere

June 22, 2024
blog

It is easy to take for granted the craftsmanship that goes into making some of the world's most beautiful luxury products, which makes it all the more fascinating when you have the opportunity to see the process for yourself. For this reason, heritage Scottish label Johnstons of Elgin has opened up its atelier – or more accurately, its mill – to share with us the extensive work that the team undertakes for every scarf, coat, knit or blanket it produces.

"Making beautiful cashmere is a result of an accumulation of so many different processes, each of them vital to the finished piece, that it’s very difficult to replicate excellence without a deep experience of working with the material," says CEO Chris Gaffney. "At Johnstons we have built up that savoir faire over centuries and because all of the steps can take place in our own mills, we can ensure quality end to end."

Johnstons – which was established back in 1797 and first brought the cashmere process to the UK in 1851 – has one of the last few vertical mills in the country which takes raw cashmere all the way through to the finished product. While certain parts of the process have been modernised over the years, it is still a long and arduous journey, which involves over 30 processes where the cashmere will be dyed, blended, spun, woven and knitted.

Johnstons of Elgin

"It all starts with sourcing the best fibre," Gaffney explains. "Our cashmere comes from Mongolia and China (Inner-Mongolia) and we buy material certified by the Sustainable Fibre Alliance to support the welfare of herders, their goats and the pastureland. We visit the pasturelands regularly and work with long-term partners who understand our needs.

Johnstons of Elgin

"Once we have the raw fibre at our mill we select the right fineness, colour and length for the particular fabric that we are creating so that when we make the yarn we are using the best possible material for the colour and weight we intend to achieve in the finished product. The creative process happens in our mills, so designers work directly with our dyers to ensure that we allow the beauty and softness of the fibre to come through in the finished product.

"When making our yarn we often blend separately dyed or natural fibres together to form new yarn colours in melange shades, increasing the depth of and vividness of colour. We can also twist yarns together to make marls or add small flecks of colour for our donegals. We then weave or knit these yarns into a multitude of complex structures, patterns and styles. The steps to make a woven cashmere scarf include dyeing, blending, carding, spinning, winding, twisting, warping, weaving, purling, washing, milling, raising, stentering, pressing, cut and sew.

"Each of these steps has sub-processes within it, every one with different choices to be made to give us the exact look and feel we are aiming for. At every stage we are checking and testing the quality. Having it all done in one place allows us to respond immediately to any issues and our teams are always pushing their colleagues to pass on the very best work along the chain."

Johnstons of Elgin

It can take up to 12 weeks for a product to get from one end of the mill to another – there are no shortcuts and everything has to be meticulously checked along the way. So much of the success of the product is down to the people who are working in the mill, Gaffney explains, who are true experts in their field.

"This whole process depends on having people that understand quality and deeply respect the product," he adds. "Being a family company that pays a real living wage, is committed to training and developing our teams and has many long-serving employees is a big part of ensuring quality."
