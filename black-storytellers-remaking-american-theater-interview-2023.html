14 Black Storytellers Who Are Remaking American Theater in 2023

March 11, 2024
blog

In 1959, Lorraine Hansberry’s A Raisin in the Sun arrived on the Great White Way, as Broadway was (unironically) nicknamed. At the time, in Montgomery, Alabama, bus segregation had recently been toppled. In Ghana, independence from Great Britain had been achieved. It was the precipice of the 1960s, a period of radical change.

It was against that backdrop that Hansberry, a 28-year-old Black woman from Chicago, made her Broadway debut with a play about a working-class family on the city’s South Side, where she had grown up. With A Raisin in the Sun, she brought three Black women to the apex of American theater: Lena Younger, the matriarch with a sense of humor and spiritual conviction; Ruth Younger, a domestic who was exploring reproductive choices and longed for a place in the world; and Beneatha, a hardheaded feminist and brilliant yet sophomoric future doctor.

It would be 17 years before another production written by a Black woman appeared on Broadway; Ntozake Shange’s for colored girls who have considered suicide/when the rainbow is enuf premiered at the Booth Theatre in the fall of 1976. Shange, who also performed in early versions of for colored girls, brought a new dramatic form to the stage: the choreopoem, which combines poetry, movement, music, and song. Seven Black female characters act as a chorus of autoethnographic voices and bodies telling deeply resonant and radically honest stories of the cruelty they’ve endured and the love they’ve carried inside their tender interior lives.

These playwrights and storytellers forged a tradition that is now flourishing in the 21st century.

Hansberry and Shange transformed American theater. They brought characters and stories—Black people with real problems and passionate dreams—that had been historically disregarded. And yet they also stood in a tradition of Black theater that had existed for decades in the shadows of the American drama scene, from the women writers and directors of historical chronicle pageants of the 1920s to the blues queens of the 1930s and the dramatists at Black colleges and universities in the pre–Civil Rights era. Together, slowly but surely, these playwrights and storytellers forged a tradition that is now flourishing in the 21st century.

Ntozake Shange (right) in the original Broadway production of her play for colored girls who have considered suicide/when the rainbow is enuf, 1976

The Spanish poet and playwright Federico García Lorca once described theater as “poetry that rises from the book and becomes human enough to talk and shout, weep and despair.” Indeed, what we have witnessed on the American stage over the past three decades is the emergence of what is now multiple generations of playwrights and dramatists who have tilled, seeded, and harvested the grounds laid by Hansberry, Shange, and so many others in order to shout and despair, but also appeal and sing.

They represent a sea change in the American theater, breaking down old boundaries.

They are Pulitzer, MacArthur, Obie, Tony, and Grammy winners. They are writers, actors, singers, composers, dancers, choreographers, and producers. They are African, Caribbean, African American, and multiracial. They embrace a great diversity of identities and experiences. They are both formally trained and self-taught. But collectively, they represent a sea change in the American theater, breaking down old boundaries around whose stories matter and who gets to tell them and how and when and where, creating space for the others who follow in their stead.

Before her illustrious career as an actor, Anna Deavere Smith collected the stories of people impacted by the 1991 riots in Crown Heights, Brooklyn, and embodied them in her 1992 one-woman play, Fires in the Mirror: Crown Heights, Brooklyn and Other Identities. A decade later, Suzan-Lori Parks became the first Black woman to win the Pulitzer Prize for Drama for Topdog/Underdog (2001), her creative remixing of the assassination of Lincoln through the lives of two contemporary Black male hustlers. Parks and Lynn Nottage—the only woman to have won the Pulitzer for Drama twice, for 2008’s Ruined and 2015’s Sweat—have each explored social conflict and the interior lives of those who experience it in their work. They both also made clear from the beginnings of their careers that they were intent on bringing not just their own narratives but their histories to the stage.

One cannot help but think of the African American folk tradition of play-acting—an organic local theater so marvelously documented by the anthropologist, playwright, and novelist Zora Neale Hurston, who recognized how the act of performing was an act of self-liberation for “the mules of the world” (a quip Hurston used to describe Black women). In 1998, Sarah Jones followed in Smith’s footsteps by using her own singular Black and female body and intellect to share the stories of victims and perpetrators of hate in her one-woman show Surface Transit.

There is a thin veil between the past and the present when we live haunted by history

Others have embarked on bodies of work that identify them as creative historians. Katori Hall’s The Mountaintop, a speculative story of Martin Luther King Jr.’s last night as a living man, catapulted her to fame in 2009. Dominique Morisseau’s Detroit cycle—Detroit ’67 (2013), Paradise Blue (2015), and Skeleton Crew (2016)—retells the story of the Black industrial North. Antoinette Chinonye Nwandu's Pass Over (2017), inspired by the killing of Trayvon Martin, meditates on contemporary Black collective grief, while Erika Dickerson-Despenza’s shadow/land (2021) and cullud wattah (2021) explore the trauma, devastation, and fallout from Hurricane Katrina in New Orleans and the Flint, Michigan, water crisis respectively.

These playwrights have also gifted American theater with a vast geography of the sort anticipated and yearned for by Hansberry and Shange, who wrote creatively and critically about West Africa and the Caribbean, respectively. Nottage, Danai Gurira, and Jackie Sibblies Drury have all depicted the stories of African women whose lives have been deeply shaped by the legacies of colonialism, neocolonialism, and patriarchy. In doing so, they’ve brought regions and histories often marginalized in the American political imagination to the center of American theatrical life. Nottage’s Ruined tells the story of a group of women during the civil war in the Democratic Republic of the Congo, while Gurira’s Eclipsed (2009) offers a window into the lives of five women amid the civil strife in Liberia. Drury’s We Are Proud to Present a Presentation About the Herero of Namibia (2012) forces us to remember the internationally “forgotten” German-led early-20th-century genocide in South West Africa.

There is an urgency but also an ancestral resonance to these stories, which are at once old and new. In 2022, nonagenarian Adrienne Kennedy finally (and much belatedly) made her Broadway debut with Ohio State Murders (1991), one of her plays focused on the life of the recurring fictional character Suzanne Alexander—an intelligent, manic, educated Black woman whose life is lightning-struck by every upheaval of the contemporary world. As creative beings, Nottage, Gurira, and Drury all understand what Suzanne feels: that there is a thin veil between the past and the present when we live haunted by history and survive against all odds, with our creative maladjustment and brilliance in tow.

A stage is a specific place, but it can carry us anywhere and everywhere. This past fall, the Harlem braid shop was transported to the Theater District in Jocelyn Bioh’s Jaja’s African Hair Braiding. Other playwrights have taken us into psychological and virtual spaces, such as Jasmine Lee-Jones with her forays into meme culture (Seven Methods of Killing Kylie Jenner, 2019) and Ife Olujobi with her commentary on consumerism and identity. (Olujobi’s new play, Jordans, premieres at New York’s Public Theater in April.)

Broadway, as it were, also isn’t the only “way.” Dramaturges such as Sade Lythcott, CEO of Harlem’s National Black Theatre project, which helped shepherd James Ijames’s Pulitzer Prize–winning Fat Ham, and Kamilah Forbes, cofounder of Hi-Arts and executive producer at the Apollo Theater, have been central to a resurgence of the Black theatrical institutional tradition. The newest kid on this particular block, Alicia Keys, is undoubtedly the most famous. A 15-time Grammy winner and multiplatinum recording artist, Keys opened her semiautobiographical musical, Hell’s Kitchen, at the Public Theater in November. With a book by Kristoffer Diaz and choreography by Camille A. Brown, it deals, in part, with her experience growing up in the orbit of Manhattan’s Theater District.

Keys has obviously distinguished herself mightily in music, but her decision to enter another field is both a singular act and one that sits fully in the experimental tradition of Black women in the performing arts. Her collaborator on Hell’s Kitchen, Brown, has herself famously refused the silos of the profession, directing the 2022 Broadway revival of Shange’s for colored girls, which was nominated for seven Tonys. Brown’s staging highlighted the cultural significance of movement in drama in a way that Shange, who also trained as a dancer, would no doubt have cherished.

As much as each of these individuals has garnered attention and acclaim, there remains a stunning silence about what it means that they are all working and thriving today.

Although it is well established that there was a late-mid-20th-century renaissance of Black women fiction writers, Black women playwrights have rarely been collectively recognized in quite the same way. Even Toni Morrison, perhaps the most influential Black American writer in history, began in the theater as a member of Howard University’s Howard Players. It is an urgent sisterhood. It seems that every week we read a new headline about one of the crises facing not just Black women but also Black femme, trans, and nonbinary people, whether it relates to maternal mortality or high rates of poverty, isolation, and discrimination. This renaissance is a literal form of fighting back with a chorus of noble self-regard.

Joan Marcus

The cast of the 2019 Public Theater production of Ntozake Shange’s for colored girls who have considered suicide/when the rainbow is enuf, choreographed by Camille A. Brown.

One could easily—and perhaps wrongly—surmise that this success means that American theater is finally integrated. But integration is a tricky word. It can simply mean that people of color cross a color line and fit themselves into a given setting without disturbing too much of what’s already there. At its best, though, integration is democratic—meaning that when more people participate in an endeavor, the very nature of it changes. Even though barriers remain, it is also true that theater just isn’t the same now that these playwrights and theater-makers have claimed space. They have expanded the idea of what theater is and can be.

James Baldwin once asked a Black woman who attended the original Broadway production of A Raisin in the Sun what made her choose to go to the theater over a movie, which was much less expensive. The woman responded that she decided to come downtown from Harlem to see the play because something was happening that had to do with her. It was a pointed, potent statement. The task these creative forces and facilitators have taken up is to pronounce that the world must have something to do with them.

Nottage has said that her interest in theater began when she listened to the stories told by Black women in her mother’s kitchen. Today, organizations such as the Lillys, a nonprofit group founded by and for women in theater, are investing in the next generation of talent through projects like the Lorraine Hansberry Initiative, which Nottage is leading along with the executive director of the Lillys, Julia Jordan, to provide graduate fellowships and support for women and nonbinary playwrights of color. The Barnard Center for Research on Women, the Public Theater, and the Ntozake Shange Literary Trust have also partnered to establish a residency for women, femme, and nonbinary playwrights of the African diaspora.

Nevertheless, writers and dramatists of those identities are still among the most underrepresented on the American stage, often unheeded, frequently stereotyped, and rarely considered, despite the intellectual robustness and tremendous diversity of their work—in content, subject, and form. But they bloom nonetheless. They insist that there is no single story that defines us, that there are myriad roads and routes through our lives. And they lead us to them with aplomb, dignity, and grace.
