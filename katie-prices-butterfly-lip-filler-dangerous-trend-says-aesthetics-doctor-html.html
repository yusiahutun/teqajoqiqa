Katie Price's 'butterfly lip filler' is a 'dangerous trend', says top aesthetics doctor

Katie Price's 'butterfly lip filler' is a 'dangerous trend', has 'unpredictable results, carries a high risk of infection, and could cause irreversible damage,' according to a top aesthetics doctor.

The mother-of-five, 44, showed off the results of her latest cosmetic treatment on Thursday, showcasing her oversized pout in a series of pictures to her 2.6million Instagram followers.

Harley Street clinic owner Dr Mohamed Hamed, whose celebrity clients include Dancing On Ice star Ekin-Su Culculoglu, Made In Chelsea's Sophie Habboo, and former Love Island contestant Faye Winter, has warned Katie's fanbase of the potential risks, insisting the procedure would not be offered by a 'reputable practitioner.'

In an exclusive interview with MailOnline, Dr Hamed, 31, who goes by the industry name Dr Motox, said: 'A reputable practitioner would not do this treatment.

'There is uproar within the industry about the butterfly lip trend. It is unsafe, unpredictable, and there is no research behind it whatsoever.

Dr Hamed has stressed that Katie could face potential 'irreversible and long-term changes' and says the method used in her Instagram stories, which includes using surgical tape to keep the shape of her lips in place, is 'dangerous' and 'unhygienic.' 

A potential complication of having your lips injected with filler, which is a hyaluronic acid gel, is it may move around or 'migrate.'

Katie's practitioner has applied a dissolver, which is a chemical typically used to break down filler, to manipulate the existing filler in her lips, before adding more, and the surgical tape maintains the plump shape.

But Dr Hamed says dissolver can take up to 72 hours to develop and it's 'impossible to predict where it will spread', meaning Katie will have no idea how her lips will look in two weeks' time.

He explained: 'I have an issue with this trend being promoted as a new and natural technique, which it isn't and it's incredibly unsafe.

'If a complication happened in my clinic, I would dissolve the filler right away. Here, you have already put dissolver in the lip so you're going to mask the signs of complications happening. It can be quite dangerous.

'Katie is going to have unpredicted results, she will not know how her lips are going to settle.

'Longterm, the filler is going to migrate again so it's defeating the point of having this treatment.

'Eventually she will need all the filler dissolved, allow it to settle and have it refilled. Then when a lip is overfilled, it will lose its elasticity and it won't be the same again.

'Usually, filler, Botox, they're reversible treatments. But this will become irreversible and cause long-term changes.'  

Dr Hamed also says the use of surgical tape in this instance is 'unhygienic' and he advices his clients to keep their mouth area impeccably clean after having a treatment to avoid risk of infection.

'The other issue here is the high risk of infection', he explained. 

'Putting tape all around your mouth, which is one of the most unhygienic areas, it harbours bacteria, blood, and you're at a much higher risk of getting infections within your lips, which again can be dangerous and could potentially spread.'
